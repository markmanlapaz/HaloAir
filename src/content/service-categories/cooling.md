## Three cooling services, one crew

AC repair, AC installation, and seasonal AC maintenance — handled by the same Red Seal technician, same warranty signature, same phone number for follow-up. Refrigerant work in Canada requires federal certification (Halo Air carries both R-410A and R-32), and we use calibrated digital manifolds and combustion analyzers on every job — not the eyeball-it gauges some shops still run.

## What goes wrong with AC, and how often

The most common AC failures we see on Halo Air repair calls — in rough order of frequency — are blown capacitors (cheap fix, often a same-visit), dirty condenser coils (cheap fix, often missed by less-thorough techs), refrigerant leaks at flare fittings (mid-cost, but topping up without finding the leak just delays the next failure by weeks), failed contactors, and frozen evaporator coils caused by low airflow. The first two are the kind of thing a spring tune-up catches; the rest tend to show up as no-cool calls in July.

The biggest installation mistake we encounter on existing systems is the wrong size — almost always oversized. An oversized AC short-cycles, fails to dehumidify properly, and chews through compressors years early. Every Halo Air install starts with a Manual J cooling load calculation done on-site.

## What customers get from a three-trade crew

A modern AC install often crosses trades: refrigerant work (Refrigeration & Air Conditioning Mechanic ticket), electrical disconnect and 240V circuit (sometimes needs an electrician), condensate drain plumbing (Plumber ticket), and any gas-line interaction with the existing furnace (Gasfitter B Ticket). Halo Air's tickets cover all but the electrical panel work itself — and we coordinate that piece with a partner electrician when it's needed, with the same written quote and same warranty.
