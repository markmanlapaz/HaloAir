## TL;DR — the two rules

1. **If the repair quote is more than ~30% of new-system cost, replace.** Lifetime cost almost always favours the new unit at that point.
2. **If the unit is 12+ years old AND uses R-22, replace.** The refrigerant is phased out and increasingly expensive. Replacement pays back fast.

Everything else needs more diagnosis. Below is the framework we use on every Halo Air call.

## Five diagnostic questions

### 1. How old is the unit?

| Age | Action |
|-----|--------|
| Under 8 years | Almost always repair — equipment is still well within its 12–18 year design life |
| 8–11 years | Mid-life — depends on repair cost and condition |
| 12–14 years | Approaching end — repair only for small fixes, lean toward replace for major work |
| 15+ years | Replace unless the repair is trivial |

Edmonton AC systems typically run 12–18 years before earning replacement. Manufacturer-rated life is usually 15 years.

### 2. What refrigerant does it use?

If your system uses **R-22** (also known as Freon), that's a major data point. R-22 was phased out and is now extremely expensive when leaks need topping up. Under Canadian regulations a top-up can only be done after the leak has been located and repaired, so an R-22 system with an active leak almost always tips toward replacement.

**R-410A** and **R-32** are the current standards. Repairs to these systems are normal and shouldn't be a red flag.

### 3. What's the repair quote?

Apply the **30% rule**: if the repair is more than roughly 30% of the cost of a comparable new system installed, replacement usually wins on lifetime cost. On every diagnostic we write both numbers — repair and replace — so the comparison is on paper before you decide.

### 4. How's the rest of the system?

A failed capacitor on a system that's otherwise healthy = repair, easy call. A failed capacitor on a system with a developing refrigerant leak, dirty coils, and a struggling blower = different story. Often the visible problem is symptom #1 of several developing issues, and the next breakdown is 6–12 months away.

A good HVAC technician will tell you what they see across the whole system — not just the one component that brought them in.

### 5. Are you planning to stay?

If you're moving in the next 2 years, repair-and-go almost always wins economically — new HVAC equipment doesn't recover 100% of its cost in resale. If you're staying 5+ years, the math shifts in favour of replacing aging equipment before it fails inconveniently, and you collect the utility savings.

## Common repair-vs-replace scenarios

**Scenario A: 8-year-old AC, blown capacitor.**
Repair. Easy call. Capacitor is a wear part, the rest of the system is fine.

**Scenario B: 13-year-old AC, refrigerant leak requiring find + fix + recharge.**
Lean replace. The cost of chasing an aging leaky system rarely beats putting in a modern unit that lasts 12–18 years and is 30%+ more efficient.

**Scenario C: 15-year-old R-22 AC, compressor failure.**
Replace. R-22 cost + compressor cost + old system = throwing money at end-of-life equipment.

**Scenario D: 9-year-old AC, contactor failed and condenser fan motor running rough.**
Repair both. Combined repair is straightforward and the equipment has 4–8 years of life left.

**Scenario E: 11-year-old AC, the third repair in 18 months.**
Evaluate carefully. The repair quote may be fine but the pattern is the signal — this system is entering end-of-life.
