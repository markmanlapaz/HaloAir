## TL;DR — the two rules

1. **If the repair quote exceeds 30% of new-system cost, replace.** For most Edmonton homes that means: repair if under ~$1,350, evaluate carefully above.
2. **If the unit is 12+ years old AND uses R-22, replace.** The refrigerant is phased out and increasingly expensive. Replacement pays back fast.

Everything else needs more diagnosis. Below is the framework we use on every Halo Air call.

## Five diagnostic questions

### 1. How old is the unit?

| Age | Action |
|-----|--------|
| Under 8 years | Almost always repair — equipment is still well within its 12–18 year design life |
| 8–11 years | Mid-life — depends on repair cost and condition |
| 12–14 years | Approaching end — repair only for small fixes, lean toward replace for major work |
| 15+ years | Replace unless the repair is trivial (under $300) |

Edmonton AC systems typically run 12–18 years before earning replacement. Manufacturer-rated life is usually 15 years.

### 2. What refrigerant does it use?

If your system uses **R-22** (also known as Freon), that's a major data point. R-22 was phased out and is now extremely expensive when leaks need topping up. Any R-22 system with a refrigerant leak is almost always a replace decision because the refrigerant cost alone exceeds the value of the fix.

**R-410A** and **R-32** are the current standards. Repairs to these systems are normal cost and shouldn't be a red flag.

### 3. What's the repair quote?

Apply the **30% rule**: if the repair is more than 30% of the cost of a comparable new system installed, replacement usually wins on lifetime cost.

Examples for an average Edmonton home (new AC ~$4,500 installed):

| Repair cost | Decision (typical) |
|-------------|---------------------|
| Under $400 | Always repair |
| $400–$1,000 | Almost always repair |
| $1,000–$1,500 | Depends on age + condition |
| $1,500–$2,500 | Lean toward replace if 10+ years |
| Over $2,500 | Almost always replace |

### 4. How's the rest of the system?

A failed capacitor on a system that's otherwise healthy = repair, easy call. A failed capacitor on a system with a developing refrigerant leak, dirty coils, and a struggling blower = different story. Often the visible problem is symptom #1 of several developing issues, and the next breakdown is 6–12 months away.

A good HVAC technician will tell you what they see across the whole system — not just the one component that brought them in.

### 5. Are you planning to stay?

If you're moving in the next 2 years, repair-and-go almost always wins economically — new HVAC equipment doesn't recover 100% of its cost in resale. If you're staying 5+ years, the math shifts in favour of replacing aging equipment before it fails inconveniently and consuming utility savings.

## Common repair-vs-replace scenarios

**Scenario A: 8-year-old AC, blown capacitor, $220 repair.**
Repair. Easy call. Capacitor is a wear part, the rest of the system is fine.

**Scenario B: 13-year-old AC, refrigerant leak, $1,800 repair (find + fix + recharge).**
Lean replace. The $1,800 buys you maybe 2–3 more years of an aging system. A $4,500 new install lasts 12–18 years and is 30%+ more efficient.

**Scenario C: 15-year-old R-22 AC, compressor failure.**
Replace. R-22 refrigerant cost + compressor cost + old system = throwing money at end-of-life equipment.

**Scenario D: 9-year-old AC, contactor failed and condenser fan motor running rough.**
Repair both — combined repair is probably $550–$750, equipment has 4–8 years of life left.

**Scenario E: 11-year-old AC, the third repair in 18 months.**
Evaluate carefully. The repair quote may be fine but the pattern is the signal — this system is entering end-of-life.
