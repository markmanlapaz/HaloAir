## TL;DR

The 10 questions that vet an Edmonton HVAC contractor in 15 minutes: **licensed?**, **insured?**, **journeyman on every job?**, **written itemized quote?**, **load calc done?**, **permits pulled by them?**, **labour warranty (years)?**, **same-day or scheduled response on warranty calls?**, **brands carried?**, **references in your neighbourhood?**. Four red flags that mean walk: **verbal-only quotes**, **"we'll sub it out"**, **no permits ("don't worry about it")**, **pressure to sign today**.

## Why this matters more than brand

The single biggest predictor of how a furnace or AC will perform over its lifetime isn't the brand. It's the install. A perfectly-installed mid-tier Goodman will outlast a poorly-installed premium Lennox every time. Bad sizing, sloppy gas pressure, undersized returns, hacked ductwork, missing combustion testing — all of these come from the contractor, not the equipment.

Spend more time vetting the contractor than vetting the brand.

## The 10 questions

### 1. Are you licensed in Alberta?

For HVAC and gas work in Alberta, your contractor needs at minimum a **Journeyman Gasfitter (B Ticket)** for gas work and a **Refrigeration & Air Conditioning Mechanic** ticket for AC/heat pump work. For full HVAC plus plumbing involvement (water heaters, hydronic), look for a Journeyman Plumber too. Halo Air carries all three.

Ask for a license number. Verify it via Alberta's Workforce Apprenticeship Board if you want to confirm.

### 2. Are you insured?

Liability insurance and WCB coverage. Both should be standard. Ask for a current Certificate of Insurance — any reputable contractor will email it within an hour of the request.

### 3. Will a journeyman be on the job from start to finish?

Apprentices are allowed to do work under journeyman supervision. They should not be unsupervised. Confirm a journeyman is on-site for combustion testing, gas pressure verification, refrigerant charging, and electrical commissioning at minimum.

### 4. Can I get a written, itemized quote?

If the answer is "we'll send you the bill" or "let's just shake on it" — walk. A real quote should list:
- Equipment (specific model)
- Labour
- Permit costs
- Materials (venting, refrigerant lines, etc.)
- Removal of old equipment
- Warranty terms
- Payment terms

Verbal-only quotes are how surprise charges happen.

### 5. Will you do a Manual J load calculation before recommending a size?

This is the question that separates real contractors from "we install whatever's on the truck" outfits. A Manual J load calculation accounts for your home's specific square footage, insulation, window count, ceiling height, basement type, and Edmonton's climate design temperature.

A contractor who "sizes by rule of thumb" or "matches what's there now" is going to oversize or undersize you. Both kill comfort and lifespan.

### 6. Do you pull the permits, or do I?

In Edmonton (and every surrounding municipality), HVAC equipment swaps require permits. The contractor should pull them. If they say "we don't usually bother with permits" — walk. This is a license issue and a warranty issue and an insurance issue all at once.

### 7. What's your labour warranty?

Manufacturer warranty is usually 10 years on parts. **Labour warranty comes from the contractor** and varies widely.

- 90 days: minimum to bother with
- 1 year: industry standard
- **2 years: what Halo Air provides** (and what we'd recommend)
- 5+ years: only some extended-protection programs offer this

### 8. What's your response time on warranty callbacks?

A 2-year warranty is only useful if they answer when you call. Ask: "If I call you 18 months from now with an install issue, how soon will someone be on-site?" Same-day or next-day should be standard for warranty work.

### 9. What brands do you carry?

A contractor who only installs one brand isn't necessarily bad — but a contractor who installs 3–4 major brands gives you flexibility. Halo Air installs Lennox, Trane, Carrier, Goodman, and Napoleon (furnaces); Lennox, Trane, Carrier, Goodman, Daikin (AC); and all major brands of tank and tankless water heaters.

### 10. Can you give me references in my neighbourhood?

A contractor who has installed in your specific area should be able to provide 2–3 nearby references. Call one. Ask: "Were they on time? Were there surprise charges? Did they clean up? Would you use them again?"

## The 4 red flags

### Red flag #1: Verbal-only quotes

If they won't put it in writing, the number isn't real.

### Red flag #2: "We'll sub that part out"

If you ask about gas-line work, electrical, or combustion testing and the answer is "we'll get our sub to look at that" — you're going to have coordination problems and warranty grey zones. One contractor responsible for the whole job is much better.

### Red flag #3: "Don't worry about permits"

This means: they're not licensed to pull them, the work won't meet code, or they're cutting corners on combustion testing. All of these are bad. Walk.

### Red flag #4: "If you sign today I can give you 20% off"

High-pressure tactics mean the price they're quoting tomorrow is the real price, and today's is fake. They're also trying to prevent you from getting a second opinion. Real contractors are confident enough in their value that they let you think it over.

## The "good fit" signs

- Asks **questions** about your comfort issues, current bills, future plans
- **Walks the whole house** before quoting, not just the mechanical room
- Talks about **trade-offs honestly** — not just "this is the best one"
- **Pulls a load calculation** before recommending a size
- Returns calls and texts on the **same day**
- Sends a **written quote within 48 hours** with specific equipment models
- Mentions **rebates and permits** proactively, not as an afterthought
