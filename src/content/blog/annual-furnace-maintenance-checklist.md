## TL;DR

Edmonton furnaces need a **professional 23-point tune-up once a year**, ideally in **September** before the cold season loads up dispatch boards. There's also a short DIY checklist you can run **monthly** during heating season. Skipping annual service can void manufacturer warranties — service documentation is required for coverage on most major brands.

## What you can (and should) do yourself

These are safe, simple, and make a real difference. Set a reminder in your calendar.

### Monthly during heating season (Oct–Apr)

- **Check the filter.** Pull it out, hold it up to a light. Can you see light through it? It's fine. Almost no light? Replace. Standard 1" filters last 1–3 months in most Edmonton homes; high-capacity 4" filters last 4–8 months.
- **Walk the supply and return vents.** Make sure none are blocked by furniture, rugs, or kids' toys. Air needs to move freely through every vent for the system to work efficiently.
- **Listen for new noises.** A furnace that's always been quiet that's suddenly clicking, banging, or grinding is telling you something — book a service call.
- **Watch the flame** (if you have a visible burner door). Should be primarily blue. Yellow flame = stop using the furnace and call for service.
- **Check around the furnace for water.** Condensate drains can clog on high-efficiency units. Water on the floor = book service.

### Once before heating season (early September)

- **Test the thermostat.** Bump it 3°C above current temp; furnace should fire within a minute.
- **Test the CO detector.** Press the test button. Replace batteries annually regardless. Detectors themselves expire — check the date on the back; most last 7–10 years.
- **Clear 3 feet around the furnace.** Combustible items (boxes, paint, cleaning supplies) should be nowhere near the furnace. Clear the area.
- **Look at the venting.** Pipes leaving the furnace should have no visible cracks, sags, or daylight at joints.

## What the professional 23-point tune-up should include

A real annual tune-up isn't a "visual inspection and a sticker on the side." Here's what to expect from a competent technician:

### Combustion & safety (8 points)

1. Burner visual inspection + cleaning
2. Heat exchanger visual + combustion analyzer test for cracks
3. Flame sensor cleaning
4. Gas pressure verification (manifold pressure)
5. Gas pressure verification (inlet pressure)
6. CO measurement at supply and return
7. Flue draft check
8. High-limit safety switch test

### Electrical & controls (5 points)

9. Capacitor microfarad test
10. Blower motor amperage draw
11. Inducer motor inspection + amperage
12. Control board diagnostics
13. Thermostat calibration

### Airflow & comfort (4 points)

14. Air filter inspection (replace if needed)
15. Blower wheel inspection (clean if accessible)
16. Static pressure measurement
17. Supply / return temperature differential

### Plumbing & drains (3 points)

18. Condensate drain flush
19. Condensate pump test (if equipped)
20. Trap clean-out

### Documentation (3 points)

21. Written condition report with photos
22. Recommended-action priority list
23. Warranty service log filed

If your service provider can't list these specifically, ask for the detailed checklist before booking.

## Why September matters

Edmonton's first cold snap usually arrives mid-to-late October. Dispatch boards at every HVAC company in the city fill up the moment furnaces start failing. By the time you call in late October needing a tune-up, you're competing for time slots with people whose furnaces stopped working.

Book in early-to-mid September and:
- You get your pick of dates
- You're not paying emergency-call rates if the technician spots a developing issue
- You have time to schedule any recommended follow-up work *before* it becomes urgent
- The technician isn't slammed and has time to do the job properly

## Why annual service preserves warranties

Almost every major furnace manufacturer (Lennox, Trane, Carrier, Goodman, Napoleon) requires documented annual professional service to honour their 10-year parts warranty. If you skip years and a major component fails in year 8, the manufacturer can decline coverage — and a repair bill that should have been free comes out of your pocket.

An annual tune-up is cheap insurance against that.
