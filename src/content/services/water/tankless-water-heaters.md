## When tankless makes sense, and when it doesn't

**Tankless is the right call when:**
- Your home has 3+ bathrooms or simultaneous high-demand fixtures (rain shower + tub + dishwasher)
- You're staying 10+ years (the longer lifespan amortizes the install premium)
- Mechanical-room space matters — tankless mounts on a wall and frees up floor space
- Your tank is in an awkward spot you'd love to repurpose
- You want longer warranty coverage (most tankless = 15-year heat exchanger vs. tank's 6-year)

**A standard tank is usually better when:**
- 1–2 person household with normal demand
- Tight install budget and the existing tank is already done
- The home has limited gas pressure and you don't want to upsize the line
- You're planning to sell in the next 3–5 years (resale buyers value reliability more than features)

We'll model both on every quote so the math is in front of you, not hidden in the upsell.

## What's different about tankless installation

Tankless isn't just "smaller tank." The installation profile is different in three ways that drive cost:

**Gas line capacity.** A 50-gallon tank typically draws 38,000–40,000 BTU/h. A residential tankless commonly draws 150,000–200,000 BTU/h — peak. The existing 1/2" gas line that fed your tank often needs upsizing to 3/4" to feed a tankless without starving it. This is the single most common reason tankless installs run over budget; we measure pressure during the quote so the answer is in writing before you commit.

**Venting.** Tankless units use sealed-combustion venting (typically 3" or 4" stainless or PVC) that runs through a sidewall or up through the roof. You can't reuse a B-vent chimney that fed an atmospheric tank — it has to be re-vented properly.

**Condensate handling.** Most modern tankless units are condensing — they extract latent heat from the flue gas, which produces acidic condensate that needs a neutralizer and a proper drain path. Skipping the neutralizer eats concrete and pipes over time.

We do all three correctly on every install. The result is a unit that actually delivers its rated flow, vents safely, and lasts the full 18–22 years.

## What's included in a tankless install

- **The unit itself** (we recommend brand/model based on flow needs and venting options)
- **Removal and disposal** of the existing water heater
- **Gas-line evaluation + upsize** if needed (quoted line-itemed up front)
- **New sealed-combustion venting** (sidewall or roof)
- **Condensate drain and neutralizer** (where the unit is condensing — most are)
- **Mounting** to wall structure with proper brackets
- **Plumbing connections** including isolation valves with service ports (essential for future descaling)
- **Electrical** plug-in connection (most tankless need a standard 120V outlet)
- **Permit** pulled with City of Edmonton (or municipality) and filed on your behalf
- **Commissioning** under load — flow rate test, gas pressure verification, vent draft check
- **Customer walkthrough** including descale procedure and error-code reference
